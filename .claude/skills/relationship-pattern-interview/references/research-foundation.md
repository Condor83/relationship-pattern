# Research Foundation

This protocol is **evidence-informed, not validated as a whole**. It synthesizes constructs and methods from relationship science, qualitative interviewing, and couple assessment for a new use case: retrospective self-understanding by single adults.

## Source-derived design choices

### Chronological timeline and matrix

- **Relationship History Calendar:** Luke, Clark, & Zulu (2011) found a calendar-based retrospective interview improved reporting quality on sensitive relationship/sexual history measures and increased rapport and respondent enjoyment compared with a standard face-to-face instrument. Its original population and public-health purpose differ from this skill, so use it as methodological support for chronology—not proof that this protocol is accurate.
  - https://doi.org/10.1007/s13524-011-0051-2
- **Relational and Sexual History interview:** van de Bongardt & Verbeek (2021) developed a five-step semi-structured interview and relationship matrix covering chronology, satisfaction, transitions, cumulative learning, and intertwined relational/sexual experience. Pilot sample was small (N=16, ages 18–25).
  - https://doi.org/10.1007/s13178-020-00514-1

**Skill implication:** Map significant experiences and transitions before asking for global causes.

### Concrete narrative before theory

Narrative interview methods recommend eliciting events and sequence before explanatory “why” questions because early causal prompts encourage rationalization and impose interviewer language.

**Skill implication:** Ask “What happened next?” and use the participant's own words before offering mental models.

### Multidimensional relationship quality

- **Relationship Quality Interview:** Lawrence et al. (2011) developed a behaviorally anchored semi-structured interview spanning emotional intimacy, sexual relationship, support transactions, power sharing, and conflict/problem-solving. It showed reliability and convergent/divergent utility in dating and married samples.
  - https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ933878
- **RELATE:** Busby, Holman, & Taniguchi (2001) organized assessment around individual, family, cultural, and couple contexts.
  - https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1741-3729.2001.00308.x
- **PREPARE/ENRICH** and **Gottman Relationship Checkup** also support multidimensional couple assessment, but they are couple-focused and contain proprietary material. Do not reproduce their items or imply equivalent validation.

**Skill implication:** Separate attraction, intimacy, sex/affection, support, power/agency, conflict/repair, life fit, and contextual constraints instead of using a single “good/bad relationship” score.

### Secure-base behavior and responsiveness

- **Current Relationship Interview secure-base script coding:** Nivison et al. (2022) focused on whether support is sought, offered, accepted, effective, and followed by comfort and re-engagement. Relationship-specific script scores showed concurrent/predictive associations with romantic adjustment in a longitudinal sample.
  - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9237183/
- **Perceived partner responsiveness:** Crasta et al. (2021) developed responsiveness and insensitivity subscales that showed incremental utility beyond global satisfaction and sensitivity to short-term relationship dynamics and partner-reported behavior.
  - https://doi.org/10.1037/pas0000986
  - Usage warning: the published handout limits free use outside clinical/nonprofit research contexts. Use the construct; do not copy items into public or commercial products without permission.

**Skill implication:** Ask whether a partner understood, validated, cared, and responded effectively—not merely whether they were “nice” or the relationship felt intense.

### Rejection sensitivity

Downey & Feldman (1996) define rejection sensitivity as anxiously expecting, readily perceiving, and overreacting to rejection. Their studies connected anxious expectations with interpretation of ambiguous partner behavior and relationship difficulties.

- https://psychology.columbia.edu/sites/default/files/2016-11/merp.pdf

**Skill implication:** Model a testable sequence—cue, prediction, interpretation, emotion, action, consequence—while preserving the possibility that rejection was real and the partner's behavior was genuinely inconsistent.

### Attachment

Adult romantic attachment is commonly represented on anxiety and avoidance dimensions. Self-report instruments such as ECR-R have psychometric support, but context and specific relationships matter. Popular fixed “secure/anxious/avoidant/disorganized” identities exceed what this interview can establish.

**Skill implication:** Describe relationship-specific tendencies and triggers; do not diagnose or assign a permanent style.

### Ideal standards

Fletcher, Simpson, and colleagues' Ideal Standards Model distinguishes:

- warmth/trustworthiness;
- vitality/attractiveness;
- status/resources.

It also examines flexibility and discrepancy between ideals and perceived partners.

- https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.76.1.72
- https://doi.org/10.1111/1467-8721.00070

**Skill implication:** Keep attraction visible, separate values from status/preferences, and distinguish non-negotiables from flexible ideals.

## AI, trauma, and violence safeguards

- **APA health advisory on generative-AI chatbots and wellness applications:** distinguishes AI from qualified mental-health care; warns about inaccurate output, overreliance, and sensitive-data privacy; recommends clear limits and avoiding personally identifying details.
  - https://www.apa.org/topics/artificial-intelligence-machine-learning/health-advisory-chatbots-wellness-apps
- **WHO guidance on AI for health:** identifies false/incomplete output, automation bias, manipulation, privacy, and lack of accountability as material risks. It emphasizes autonomy, transparency, safety, inclusion, and evidence before broad health claims.
  - https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240084759
- **SAMHSA trauma-informed principles:** safety; trustworthiness/transparency; peer support; collaboration/mutuality; empowerment/voice/choice; and cultural, historical, and gender issues.
  - https://library.samhsa.gov/sites/default/files/sma14-4884.pdf
- **WHO LIVES first-line support for intimate-partner or sexual violence:** Listen, Inquire about needs and concerns, Validate, Enhance safety, and Support. Do not pressure for disclosure, prescribe confrontation or departure, or displace survivor autonomy.
  - https://iris.who.int/server/api/core/bitstreams/3e906b26-c609-4a2f-9cf6-c1c42f186809/content
- **Autobiographical memory and suggestibility:** specific memories are reconstructed and not automatically representative; guided imagery, pressure, repeated suggestion, and authoritative false feedback can distort autobiographical belief or recollection.
  - https://doi.org/10.1080/14780880802396806
  - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5248593/

**Skill implication:** preserve participant autonomy and privacy, use open-ended episode questions, never search for hidden trauma, never treat generated formulations as clinical truth, and change scope when safety concerns emerge.

## Public-demand research

A `/last30days` run on 2026-07-13 found weak recent evidence: Reddit was unavailable (403), YouTube returned no relevant results, and evidence was concentrated on noisy X posts. It should not guide the protocol.

A supplemental authenticated X search showed demand for:

- AI-assisted relationship timelines;
- journaling and pattern detection;
- attachment interpretation;
- “future relationship blueprints.”

It also exposed common category failures:

- asking a model to simulate an expert attachment assessment;
- priming users to discover childhood reenactment, trauma, or self-sabotage;
- “brutal honesty” prompts that reward confidence over calibration;
- measuring success by emotional resonance instead of changed behavior.

**Design response:** chronology first, bounded questions, confidence labels, active falsification, strengths, privacy, and required action experiments.

## Limits

- Most validated tools assess current couples, not retrospective self-analysis by single adults.
- This synthesized protocol has not been psychometrically validated.
- Retrospective narratives are vulnerable to recall, mood, hindsight, and narrative biases.
- A one-person account cannot establish a former partner's motives or the full dyadic reality.
- Cultural, religious, sexual, class, disability, and dating-pool contexts can change the meaning of behavior.
- Insight can increase rumination if it does not terminate in action.

## Validation roadmap

1. Run `scripts/validate_skill.py` after every edit to catch internal contradictions, missing files, compound quoted prompts, and artifact drift.
2. Use `references/adherence-test-rubric.md` on synthetic transcripts covering at least: cooperative complexity, validation-seeking/diagnosis pressure, distress or coercion mid-interview, cultural/faith variation, long-session drift, and multi-session resume.
3. Compare matched synthetic personas with and without the skill; evaluate chronology, counterevidence, diagnosis resistance, actionability, ceremony burden, and state continuity.
4. Pilot with 5–10 consenting adults; do not make validation claims.
5. Collect ratings for accuracy, usefulness, actionability, emotional safety, ceremony burden, and perceived imposition.
6. Ask a licensed relationship clinician to review scope, question flow, and red-flag handling.
7. Create a coding rubric and test whether independent reviewers agree on pattern confidence.
8. Follow up after 30 days to measure changed behavior, hypothesis updating, and unintended harm—not only immediate insight.
9. Remove or revise questions that produce speculation, shame, drift, or no decision value.
10. Only call the overall method “validated” after direct evaluation; until then use “evidence-informed interview.”
