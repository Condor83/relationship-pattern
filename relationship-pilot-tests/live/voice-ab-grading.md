# Voice Layer A/B Grading — relationship-pattern-interview

- **Test:** A/B comparison of the v0.5.0 "Interviewer stance" voice layer (SKILL.md `### Interviewer stance` + `references/example-exchanges.md`) against v0.4.0 (no voice layer)
- **Date:** 2026-07-13
- **Arms:**
  - CONTROL — v0.4.0, `relationship-pilot-tests/live/ab-control-transcript.md`
  - VOICE — v0.5.0 candidate, `relationship-pilot-tests/live/ab-voice-transcript.md`
- **Persona:** scripted "Casey" (38, teacher, Portland) with four identical scripted pressure beats; no safety content. Graded against `references/adherence-test-rubric.md`, sections A and D emphasized.
- **Grader:** independent evaluator; had no part in producing either transcript. Not blind to arm labels (see Limitations).

## Verdict

**PASS.** The voice arm is equal-or-better than control on rubric section A (both openings are exactly 129 words and protocol-complete, but control committed the section's one substantive failure by bundling Q7 into checkpoint 1, while the voice arm's only A-side blemishes are marginal: checkpoint bullets at 130 words vs the ~120 guideline and a 4-sentence route briefing whose extra "sentence" is the three-word fragment "Here's the route"). It is equal-or-better on section D (both arms correctly refuse the self-sabotage verdict and handle "that's not pathetic, right?" without pathologizing or cheerleading; the voice arm's refusal is cleaner and answers the "tell me straight" framing on its own terms). It is clearly better on three voice dimensions: crying-disclosure handling (control converted the disclosure into a virtue; voice acknowledged it plainly), praise budget (control ~7 praise instances including at least three attached to agreement or disclosure-effort — a direct violation of the stance rule; voice 4 instances, all attached to specificity/precision/evidence handling), and register consistency (voice sounds like one dry-precise examiner from turn 1 to turn 8; control oscillates between effusive and counselor-adjacent). No protocol regression was found in the voice arm — it was also 11% less wordy overall. The voice layer earns its ship, with two wording notes for iteration listed at the end.

### 3. Jargon

- VOICE: no clinical or self-help vocabulary; "behavioral," "testable," "hypotheses" are protocol terms. Pass.

**Tie.** Both arms clean.

### 4. Exclamation and enthusiasm inflation

- Exclamation marks (interviewer turns): CONTROL **0**, VOICE **0** (mechanically counted).
- Interviewer superlatives: CONTROL 3 — "one of the most useful destination statements I've heard" (inflationary: implies cross-participant flattery), "the kind of case that teaches us the most" (analytic), "the most important open thread" (analytic). VOICE 2 — "the most testable of the three" (analytic, bounded), "the most precise thing you've said so far" (bounded to session).

**Slight voice edge** (one inflationary superlative in control, none in voice); not counted as "clearly better."

### 5. Hedging stacks

- CONTROL T4: "I'm just marking that contrast for now; we'll test what it means against your actual history, not guess" + "One thing I want to keep loose until you tighten it" — two hedges in one turn, each single.
- VOICE T6: "I'm also noting two things for later **without interpreting them yet**… Both are **data, not verdicts**" — a double qualifier where one would do.
- Both arms perform the mark-don't-interpret ritual about equally often; neither stacks three-deep ("maybe/perhaps/possibly") anywhere.

**Tie.**

### 6. Register consistency

- CONTROL drifts: witty-examiner (T3 "taking it seriously before your calendar does"), effusive (T4 "one of the most useful… I've heard"), disclosure-warm (T6 "Thanks for pushing through the squirm"), and counselor-adjacent at close (T8 "sitting with it is real work too… if Sunday 11pm shows up before then: the state of the evidence is 'unknown, with named strengths' — not 'doomed.'"). Each is individually defensible; together they read as three interviewers.
- VOICE holds one dry-precise register from first turn to last: "Locked in." (T2), "Filed, not concluded." (T4), "Same file." (T5), "It's going on the board." (T7), "Sit with it as long as you need. Anything in there I've misstated?" (T8). Early and late turns are indistinguishable in voice.

**Clearly better: VOICE.**

## Notes for iteration (non-blocking)

1. **Checkpoint word budget:** voice checkpoint bullets ran 130 words vs the rubric's "about 120." The stance's own example (example-exchanges.md #5) is far tighter; consider adding "bullets ≤120 words" to the checkpoint line in the stance so the example's compactness survives contact with a rich Phase 1.
2. **Route briefing sentence count:** the voice arm's "Here's the route." fragment plus three sentences technically exceeds the rubric's three-sentence cap even while using fewer words than control. Either let the rubric say "three sentences (~110 words)" or fold the fragment into the first sentence in example guidance.
3. Control's checkpoint/Q7 bundling suggests the base protocol text still permits reading "checkpoint + next question" as one turn; the voice arm happened to get it right, but a one-line clarification in SKILL.md ("the checkpoint turn ends at the choice") would make this arm-independent.

## Limitations

- **Single run per arm.** One transcript each; every observed difference could partly be sampling noise (e.g., control's Q7 bundling might not recur, and the voice arm's discipline might not either).
- **Same underlying model in both arms**, so this measures the marginal effect of prompt text, not robustness across models.
- **Scripted persona with improvised filler:** the four pressure beats were identical verbatim, but Casey's surrounding answers diverged between arms (control: Sellwood apartment, "museum exhibit," Sunday farmers-market ritual; voice: sister/Dana theories, "thermostat," solitude-hoarding). Some interviewer differences therefore reflect different inputs, not only the voice layer — notably the voice arm's crisper self-sabotage answer benefited from Casey having named her sister's and Dana's theories in that arm.
- **Grader not blind to arm labels** (file names and version headers identify the arms), which risks confirmation bias despite verbatim-citation discipline.
- No safety content was exercised; this run says nothing about the voice layer's interaction with section F behavior.
